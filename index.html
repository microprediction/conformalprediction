Marginally Useful

Conformal prediction certifies the coverage of a set. It does not estimate a distribution. These demos show you the difference.

Conformal prediction is real mathematics with a real guarantee. The trouble is that the guarantee — marginal coverage — is usually not the thing people reach for it to provide. This site is an honest, interactive tour: first we build the method and watch it work, then we watch it answer a question you probably weren’t asking.

Given exchangeable data, a predictor, and a nonconformity score, split conformal prediction returns a set \(C(X)\) with $$\mathbb{P}\big(Y_{n+1}\in C(X_{n+1})\big)\;\ge\;1-\alpha.$$ Read the probability carefully: the average is over \(X_{n+1}\) too. It is a statement about the average input — not about the one in front of you.

Every claim below is something you can move a slider and see for yourself. The companion expository paper states the same facts precisely, with citations and three small contributions.

The fence is the horizon

A deliberately useless predictor reaches exactly 90% coverage. Validity is not evidence of a good method.
04

Coverage ⊥ log-score

Pin marginal coverage at 90% and move the predictive log-likelihood up and down at will. The two live on independent axes.
05

Exchangeability & time series

Turn on drift and the one true guarantee evaporates. The adaptive patch restores only a long-run average, never coverage now.
06

Conformal vs. recalibration

If you actually wanted a calibrated forecast, distributional recalibration improves the log-score and hands back a density. Conformal just re-levels.

Why you can’t just fix it: the no-go results

The gaps in Demos 02–05 are not bugs to be patched — they are theorems. Distribution-free conditional coverage is provably unavailable except by surrendering the very thing that made the interval useful. These two demos make the impossibility tangible.

07 · no-go

The price of conditional coverage

Chase per-\(x\) coverage by localizing, and watch the intervals diverge to the whole real line. Lei & Wasserman (2014): conditional validity forces infinite expected length.
08 · no-go

Subgroup coverage buys only a wider band

Protect every subgroup distribution-free and all you get is a flat band at the inflated level \(1-\alpha\delta\) — wider, never adaptive. Barber et al. (2021), Theorem 3.1.

Does it help in practice? A real benchmark

Rhetoric is cheap, so there is also an empirical benchmark that puts the actual libraries — MAPIE, crepes — and the strong time-series conformal methods on trial against probabilistic baselines and an oracle, scored by conditional coverage and proper scores. The honest finding: the adaptive conformal skins genuinely help, but they help because of the conditional model they wrap — raw quantile regression with no conformal step ties conformalized quantile regression — while the conformal layer supplies the marginal certificate on top. The one-paragraph version

Forecast quality decomposes into calibration (do stated probabilities match observed frequencies?) and sharpness (how concentrated is the prediction?), and a proper scoring rule grades both. Conformal prediction touches only the marginal, coverage-flavored slice of calibration, and is constitutionally blind to sharpness, because the width of its set is inherited wholesale from the base model. So you cannot improve a distribution by conformalizing it — the estimation was already done by the score; conformal merely re-levels. Asking it to “estimate the distribution better” is asking the calibration knob to do the modeling.

If you remember one sentence: conformal prediction certifies the coverage of a set; it does not estimate a distribution. Confusing the two is a category error — and coverage answers a question, is the truth in this region?, that in forecasting you were usually not asking.

When conformal prediction is the right tool

This site is skeptical, not dismissive. There is a clean litmus test: is your loss a function of whether the truth lands in a region, or of where it lands? If the former — selective prediction with a recall guarantee, retrieval shortlists, anomaly detection as a distribution-free test, compliance certificates — coverage is the native object and conformal prediction is exactly right. Demo 06 and the paper’s §7 lay out the honest steelman.
