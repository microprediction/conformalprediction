\begin{abstract}
We give the validity of split conformal prediction an exact contragredient form: coverage is an
orbit average, and the Reynolds identity $\langle P^{\vee}f,v\rangle=\langle f,Pv\rangle$ lets
it be read either as transporting the evaluation functional or as averaging the data. The primal
construction under the same permutation action is Steinitz balancing: ordering a zero-sum
population so that every prefix sum stays bounded. Conformal placements form such a population,
and we construct an explicit ordering whose running coverage stays within $1/(2t)$ of nominal,
exact on the full orbit. On the function--measure pairing, the literal transpose of calibration
is balanced sampling and exact cubature. Exchangeability is untouched: it alone makes the
designated test placement an even draw.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Split conformal prediction validates its coverage claim by a symmetry argument over sample
slots. This note gives that argument an exact dual form and identifies the deterministic
construction that lives beside it under the same group action. The identity behind conformal
validity is a contragredient statement for the permutation action on slots
(Section~\ref{sec:contra}). The primal construction under that action is \emph{Steinitz
balancing}: ordering a finite zero-sum population so that every prefix sum stays uniformly
bounded \citep{steinitz1913}. Conformal placements are themselves a scalar Steinitz population
(Section~\ref{sec:balanced}), and admit a sharper balanced ordering than the general lemma
provides. On the function--measure pairing, the literal transpose of calibration is balanced
sampling and exact cubature (Section~\ref{sec:cubature}). Section~\ref{sec:bp} records how the
same two-ingredient structure appears in the dependent-data floor of
\citet{barberpananjady2026}, and Section~\ref{sec:rates} separates three rates that the shared
algebra can blur. As far as we know, the connection between conformal placement and balanced
orderings has not been drawn before. Herding \citep{welling2009}, whose $O(1/n)$ bookkeeping
resembles the constructions here, is the with-replacement, continuous-domain relaxation of the
finite balanced-permutation problem \citep{harvey2014}; it appears only in
Remark~\ref{rmk:herding}.

\section{Conformal calibration and placement}\label{sec:conformal}

Fix $\alpha\in(0,1)$, write $p=1-\alpha$, let $S_1,\dots,S_n$ be calibration scores and
$S_{n+1}$ a test score, almost surely distinct, and let $k=\lceil p\,(n+1)\rceil\le n$. Write
$N=n+1$. Split conformal declares coverage when $S_{n+1}\le q_n$, with $q_n=S_{(k)}$ the $k$-th
smallest calibration score.

\begin{lemma}[Steering identity]\label{lem:steer}
With $q_n=S_{(k)}$, $h_q(s)=\ind{s\le q}$, and the scores distinct,
\[
\sum_{i=1}^{n}\bigl(h_{q_n}(S_i)-p\bigr)\;=\;k-pn\;\in\;[\,p,\;1+p\,).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Exactly $k$ calibration scores satisfy $S_i\le S_{(k)}$; and $p(n+1)\le k<p(n+1)+1$.
\end{proof}

The material of this section is standard \citep{papadopoulos2002,vovk2005,lei2018}.

\begin{proposition}[Counting identity]\label{prop:count}
Let $s_1,\dots,s_{N}$ be distinct reals and, for $i\in\{1,\dots,N\}$, call placement $i$
\emph{covered} if $s_i$ is at most the $k$-th smallest of $\{s_j\}_{j\ne i}$, writing
$\chi_i(s)=\ind{s_i\le Q_k(s_{-i})}$ for the indicator. Then $\chi_i(s)=1$ iff the rank of $s_i$
among all $N$ values is at most $k$, and consequently $\sum_{i=1}^{N}\chi_i(s)=k$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Placement $i$ is covered iff at most $k-1$ of the other values are smaller than $s_i$, iff its
overall rank is at most $k$; ranks are a permutation of $\{1,\dots,N\}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Marginal validity]\label{cor:valid}
If $(S_1,\dots,S_{N})$ is exchangeable with almost surely distinct components, then
$\Prob\{S_{N}\le q_n\}=k/N\ge 1-\alpha$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The coverage event is the event that placement $N$ is covered. By exchangeability every
placement indicator has the same expectation; average Proposition~\ref{prop:count}.
\end{proof}

The control-theoretic reading of the calibration step is established in the online literature:
the level update of adaptive conformal inference \citep{gibbscandes2021} is integral feedback on
realized miscoverage, and \citet{angelopoulos2023pid} name the identification. There the
controlled quantity is a time average along a sequence; Lemma~\ref{lem:steer} is the batch
counterpart, a single dead-beat correction.

\section{The contragredient identity}\label{sec:contra}

Let $V=E^{N}$ for a vector space $E$, with $\mathfrak S_{N}$ acting by permuting slots,
$(\rho(\pi)v)_j=v_{\pi^{-1}(j)}$, and let $\rho^{\vee}(\pi)f=f\circ\rho(\pi^{-1})$ be the
contragredient action on functionals (for the dual representation and the averaging operator see, e.g., \citealp{serre1977}). For the prefix-sum functionals
$L_t(v)=\sum_{j=1}^{t}v_j$ one has, exactly,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:contra}
L_t\bigl(\rho(\pi)v\bigr)\;=\;\bigl(\rho^{\vee}(\pi^{-1})L_t\bigr)(v):
\end{equation}
permute the points holding the prefix functional fixed, or hold the points fixed and permute the
functional. Averaging over the group, let $P=\frac1{N!}\sum_{\pi}\rho(\pi)$ be the Reynolds
projector and $P^{\vee}=\frac1{N!}\sum_{\pi}\rho^{\vee}(\pi)$ its dual, so that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:reynolds}
\langle P^{\vee}f,\,v\rangle\;=\;\langle f,\,Pv\rangle
\qquad\text{for all } f\in V^{*},\ v\in V .
\end{equation}
For the permutation representation, $Pv=\bar v\,\mathbf 1$ and
$P^{\vee}e^{*}_{N}=\frac1N\mathbf 1^{*}$.

This is the formal statement behind the two descriptions of conformal validity. Define the
centered placement residual vector $r(s)=(\chi_i(s)-k/N)_{i\le N}$; Proposition~\ref{prop:count}
says $\langle\mathbf 1^{*},r(s)\rangle=0$, i.e.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:orbitzero}
\langle P^{\vee}e^{*}_{N},\,r(s)\rangle\;=\;0
\qquad\text{for every distinct } s .
\end{equation}
Exchangeability then converts the orbit average into the law of the designated placement:
$\E\,\chi_{N}(S)=\E\,\langle P^{\vee}e^{*}_{N},\chi(S)\rangle=k/N$, which is
Corollary~\ref{cor:valid}. The identity is exact and requires no analogy; it is the defining
identity of the dual representation, applied to the acceptance vector. What it does not do is
remove exchangeability: \eqref{eq:orbitzero} is a statement about the whole orbit, and only
exchangeability makes the one designated test placement uniform on it.

\section{Steinitz balancing}\label{sec:steinitz}

The primal construction living under the same $\mathfrak S_{N}$ action is the ordering of a
finite zero-sum population. If $z_1,\dots,z_{N}\in E$ satisfy $\sum_i z_i=0$ and $\|z_i\|\le 1$,
the Steinitz lemma \citep{steinitz1913} supplies a permutation $\pi$ with every prefix bounded,
$\|\sum_{j\le t}z_{\pi(j)}\|\le C_E$ for all $t$, with $C_E$ depending on the space and norm but
not on $N$ (a dimension-linear bound holds for arbitrary norms; \citealp{grinberg1980}); hence running averages within $C_E/t$ of zero. This is the bounded-ledger
$O(1/t)$ phenomenon, obtained by an element of the same permutation group that
Section~\ref{sec:contra} acts with, and its dual operation really is the transport of a prefix
functional through \eqref{eq:contra}.

For the relation to herding, see Remark~\ref{rmk:herding}.

\section{Balanced conformal placements}\label{sec:balanced}

Conformal placements are themselves a scalar Steinitz population: by
Proposition~\ref{prop:count} the residuals $r_i(s)=\chi_i(s)-k/N$ satisfy $\sum_i r_i(s)=0$ and
$|r_i(s)|\le 1$. Because they take only two values, the ordering can be made sharper than the
general lemma provides.

\begin{proposition}[Balanced placement ordering]\label{prop:balanced}
For every distinct score vector $s$ there is a permutation $\pi\in\mathfrak S_{N}$ such that,
simultaneously for every $t=1,\dots,N$,
\[
\Bigl|\sum_{j=1}^{t}\chi_{\pi(j)}(s)-\frac{tk}{N}\Bigr|\;\le\;\frac12,
\qquad\text{hence}\qquad
\Bigl|\frac1t\sum_{j=1}^{t}\chi_{\pi(j)}(s)-\frac{k}{N}\Bigr|\;\le\;\frac{1}{2t},
\]
with equality to zero at $t=N$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Define the balanced binary word
$b_t=\lfloor tk/N+\tfrac12\rfloor-\lfloor (t-1)k/N+\tfrac12\rfloor$. Since $0\le k/N\le 1$,
each $b_t\in\{0,1\}$, and $\sum_{j\le t}b_j=\lfloor tk/N+\tfrac12\rfloor$, so
$|\sum_{j\le t}b_j-tk/N|\le\tfrac12$ for every $t$, with exactly $k$ ones among
$b_1,\dots,b_N$. Assign the $k$ covered placement indices to the positions with $b_t=1$ and the
uncovered indices to the rest.
\end{proof}

This is one-dimensional balanced rounding, performed on the conformal placement orbit itself;
prefix bounds of this type are classical, going back to the chairman assignment problem
\citep{tijdeman1980}. In the notation of Section~\ref{sec:contra} the two readings
are literal:
$\langle L_t,\rho(\pi)r(s)\rangle=\langle\rho^{\vee}(\pi^{-1})L_t,r(s)\rangle$ --- move the
residual vector through the permutation holding the prefix test fixed, or hold the residuals
fixed and move the nested family of prefix functionals.

What the proposition means, and what it does not. It gives a deterministic traversal of the $N$
possible evaluation placements whose running coverage is calibrated to $O(1/t)$: useful for
orbit computations and repeated-placement audits. It does not replace exchangeability in
one-shot conformal prediction, where a single placement is designated as the test; only
exchangeability makes that placement uniform on the orbit.

\section{Balanced sampling: the function--measure transpose}\label{sec:cubature}

If the pairing of interest is $\langle h,\nu\rangle=\int h\,d\nu$ rather than the permutation
representation, the literal transpose of calibration is balanced sampling. Calibration solves:
given the empirical score measure $\nu_n$, find a threshold test $h_q$ with
$\langle h_q,\nu_n\rangle=k/n$. Its transpose solves: given tests $h_1,\dots,h_d$ and targets
$m_1,\dots,m_d$, find a measure $\nu$ with $\langle h_j,\nu\rangle=m_j$. Over a finite candidate
population with $A_{ji}=h_j(x_i)$ this is the selection problem $Az=b$, $z\in\{0,1\}^N$, which
is precisely the balanced-sampling problem solved (exactly when geometry and integrality
permit, approximately otherwise) by the cube method \citep{devilletille2004}. For weighted
atomic measures, convex geometry does better: $\mu=\int\phi\,dP$ lies in the convex hull of
$\phi(\mathcal X)$, so Carath\'eodory supplies at most $d+1$ weighted points matching all $d$
moments exactly, the Tchakaloff side of exact cubature \citep{tchakaloff1957}. In this
formulation, balanced sampling and exact cubature are the dual problem.

A designed calibration set sits inside this frame. If designed scores $S_1,\dots,S_n$ have
empirical distribution function within Kolmogorov distance $D$ of a continuous reference law
$\bar F$, and the test score is drawn from $\bar F$ independently of the design, then
$|\bar F(S_{(k)})-k/(n+1)|\le D+1/(n+1)$ and $|\bar F(S_{(k)})-p|<D+(1+p)/n$, deterministically
in the design (the proof is $\hat F_n(S_{(k)})=k/n$ plus Lemma~\ref{lem:steer}). A quantile
grid achieves $D\le 1/(2n)$. This is design-based rather than distribution-free: it suppresses
the $n^{-1/2}$ fluctuation of conditional coverage at the price of assuming the target law, and
whether reproducing-kernel guarantees of kernel herding or kernel thinning
\citep{chen2010,dwivedi2021} control the Kolmogorov discrepancy required here is left open.

\section{The Barber--Pananjady floor}\label{sec:bp}

The two-ingredient structure of the preceding sections reappears in the dependent-data coverage
floor of \citet{barberpananjady2026}. For $S=(S_1,\dots,S_{N})$ they define subvectors
$\Delta^{0}_{k,\tau}(S)$ and $\Delta^{1}_{k,\tau}(S)$ of length $N-\tau$, deleting a block of
$\tau$ entries while keeping $S_{N}$ (respectively rotating $S_k$) in the final slot, and set
\[
\Psi_{k,\tau}(S)=d_{\mathrm{TV}}\bigl(\mathcal L(\Delta^{0}_{k,\tau}(S)),
\mathcal L(\Delta^{1}_{k,\tau}(S))\bigr),
\qquad
\bar\Psi_\tau=\frac1{N}\sum_{k=1}^{N}\Psi_{k,\tau}(S),
\]
with $d_{\mathrm{TV}}(P,Q)=\sup_A|P(A)-Q(A)|$. Their Theorem~5 gives, for a pretrained
memoryless score,
\[
\Prob\{S_{N}\le q_n\}\;\ge\;1-\alpha-\min_{0\le\tau\le n}
\Bigl\{\tfrac{\tau}{N}+\bar\Psi_\tau\Bigr\},
\]
their Corollary~6 bounds $\bar\Psi_\tau\le 2\beta(\tau)$ under stationary $\beta$-mixing, their
Theorem~8 supplies a matching ceiling, and their Theorem~7 shows tightness up to the factor
$(1-\alpha)/4$.

The argument separates into an exact insertion--deletion stability statement for empirical
quantile \emph{levels} (deleting $\tau$ entries moves the rank of a fixed threshold by at most
$\tau$, so the level shifts by at most $\tau/N$: order-statistic geometry, controlling levels
rather than values, since empirical quantile values are not stable under small total-variation
perturbations) and a total-variation comparison between switched laws, which is the definition
of the switch coefficient. Both ingredients are linear in their perturbation sizes and no
concentration step appears; a blocking argument \citep{oliveira2024} bounds the same quantity
through a maximal inequality and pays $\sqrt{\tau/n}$.

\section{Three rates}\label{sec:rates}

The shared algebra can blur three quantities. The marginal coverage $k/N$ of
Corollary~\ref{cor:valid} is an exact symmetry identity; its $O(1/n)$ deviation from $p$ is
rank-grid rounding, not estimation error. The conditional coverage $\bar F(q_n)$ of a randomly
sampled calibration set fluctuates around nominal at the sampling scale $n^{-1/2}$; the design
of Section~\ref{sec:cubature} suppresses this at the price of assuming $\bar F$. Steinitz discrepancies
are genuine approximation errors, $O(1/t)$ with constants from the Steinitz lemma. The ledgers match; the objects
differ.

\section{Remarks}\label{sec:remarks}

\begin{remark}[The pinball dual]
Threshold calibration also has an exact convex dual. The empirical $p$-quantile minimises the pinball loss \citep{koenker1978},
$\sum_i\rho_p(S_i-q)$ with $\rho_p(r)=\sup_{u\in[p-1,p]}ur$, and Fenchel duality gives
\[
\max\Bigl\{\textstyle\sum_i u_iS_i:\ u_i\in[p-1,p],\ \sum_i u_i=0\Bigr\},
\]
with $u_i=p-h_q(S_i)$ away from the threshold tie. The dual optimum is a balanced signed
measure on the scores: calibration's exact optimization dual, though not itself a
point-generation algorithm.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Herding]\label{rmk:herding}
Herding \citep{welling2009} generates points by the recursion $w_t=w_{t-1}+\mu-\phi(x_t)$,
$x_t=\arg\max_x\langle w_{t-1},\phi(x)\rangle$, and attains $O(1/n)$ feature-mean discrepancy
conditional on bounded weights; the identification with Frank--Wolfe \citep{bach2012} shows the
fast rate needs interiority-type conditions, with \citet{chen2010} treating the kernel case.
\citet{harvey2014} pose herding over a finite population as exactly the balanced-permutation
problem of Section~\ref{sec:steinitz}, making generic herding the with-replacement relaxation of
Steinitz balancing; on the pairing of Section~\ref{sec:cubature} it is one iterative approximate
solver for balanced sampling. The quadrature triangle is older on that side: optimally-weighted
herding is Bayesian quadrature \citep{huszar2012}, and \citet{snellgriffiths2025} recently
recast the conformal computation as Bayesian quadrature over the calibration scores.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Limits]
The passage from orbit identity to one-shot coverage is exchangeability's, or its mixing
surrogate's in Section~\ref{sec:bp}; no bookkeeping supplies it. Nor does control of thresholds
and labels create information: it re-levels coverage while conditional sharpness remains a
property of the scores \citep{cotton_marginally}. Whether placement balancing can be aimed
conditionally, selecting or reweighting calibration slots to balance the rank count given the
input, is open; localized and weighted conformal methods are the nearest existing steps.
\end{remark}
