\title{Moving the Slots, Not the Balls:\\
Conformal Prediction as the Contragredient of Herding}
\author{Peter Cotton\\\texttt{peter.cotton@microprediction.com}}
\date{July 2026}

\begin{abstract}
Herding constructs a point set whose empirical feature averages track a target measure: it
\emph{moves the particles} against fixed test functionals, and when its weight sequence stays
bounded it earns an $O(1/n)$ discrepancy where i.i.d.\ sampling gives $O(1/\sqrt n)$. We observe
that the coverage argument behind split conformal prediction is the dual operation. The realized
scores are never touched; the proof transports the \emph{designation} ``this slot holds the test
point'' across the index set, and because the empirical quantile is a symmetric function of the
scores, a deterministic counting identity holds: exactly $\lceil(1-\alpha)(n+1)\rceil$ of the $n+1$
possible placements of that designation point at covered scores. In the roulette image: herding
moves the balls to balance a fixed wheel; conformal holds the balls fixed and rotates the slot
labels under the pointer. We make the duality precise as a statement about the permutation action
on score vectors and its contragredient action on coordinate functionals, and we use it to read
the switch-coefficient analysis of \citet{barberpananjady2026} as index-space low-discrepancy
accounting: deleting $\tau$ slots damages the rank count by at most $\tau$, worth $\tau/n$ of
coverage, and the only probabilistic price is the total-variation failure of rotation invariance,
$\bar\Psi_\tau \le 2\beta(\tau)$. The linear $1/n$ rates on both sides are the signature of
counting rather than concentration, which is why they beat the $\sqrt{\tau/n}$ obtained when the
counting structure is abandoned for blocking and empirical processes. We are explicit about where
the steering sits: conformal \emph{does} perform feedback control --- the empirical quantile moves
the acceptance boundary until the accumulated acceptance-rate error is bounded by a single unit
--- but the control acts in the dual space of thresholds, acceptance sets, and index labels, never
on the generation of observations, and the probabilistic work is still done by exchangeability or
its mixing surrogate.
\end{abstract}

\section{Two mechanisms}\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Herding moves the balls.}
Fix a feature map $\phi:\mathcal X\to\mathcal H$ and a target expectation
$\mu=\E_{P}\,\phi(X)$. Herding \citep{welling2009} generates points deterministically,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:herding}
x_{t}\;=\;\arg\max_{x\in\mathcal X}\,\langle w_{t-1},\,\phi(x)\rangle,
\qquad
w_{t}\;=\;w_{t-1}+\mu-\phi(x_{t}),
\end{equation}
so that the weight vector $w_t = w_0 + t\mu - \sum_{s\le t}\phi(x_s)$ is, up to initialization,
$t$ times the running discrepancy between the target mean and the empirical feature mean. If the
dynamics keep $\|w_t\|$ bounded --- guaranteed in finite dimension under mild conditions, and more
delicately in a general RKHS \citep{chen2010,bach2012} --- then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:herdingrate}
\Bigl\|\tfrac1n\textstyle\sum_{t=1}^{n}\phi(x_t)-\mu\Bigr\|
\;=\;\frac{\|w_n-w_0\|}{n}\;=\;O\!\left(\frac1n\right),
\end{equation}
a quasi--Monte Carlo rate, against the $O(n^{-1/2})$ of i.i.d.\ sampling. The tests $\phi$ are
held fixed; the \emph{state-space locations} of the particles are what the algorithm manipulates.
\citet{bach2012} identify \eqref{eq:herding} with the Frank--Wolfe algorithm on the marginal
polytope, which is where the boundedness conditions live; \citet{chen2010} read it as greedy
quasi--Monte Carlo.

\paragraph{Conformal moves the slots.}
Split conformal prediction \citep{vovk2005,lei2018} computes conformity scores
$S_1,\dots,S_n$ on a calibration set, a score $S_{n+1}$ on the test point, and declares coverage
when $S_{n+1}$ falls below the inflated empirical quantile
$Q_{1-\alpha}=\text{the }k\text{-th smallest of }S_1,\dots,S_n$, with
$k=\lceil(1-\alpha)(n+1)\rceil$. Nothing in the procedure moves a score. The proof of validity
instead moves a \emph{label}. Write $S=(S_1,\dots,S_{n+1})\in\R^{n+1}$ for the realized scores ---
the balls, resting where they fell --- and let $e_i^{*}$ denote the coordinate functional that
reads slot $i$. The coverage event is a statement about the pairing
$\langle e_{n+1}^{*},S\rangle$ and the symmetric function $Q_{1-\alpha}$ of the remaining
coordinates. The proof asks: what if the designation ``test'' had pointed at slot $i$ instead?

\begin{observation}[The rank count is already balanced]\label{obs:count}
Let $s_1,\dots,s_{n+1}$ be distinct reals and $k=\lceil(1-\alpha)(n+1)\rceil$. For each placement
$i\in\{1,\dots,n+1\}$, say the placement is \emph{covered} when $s_i$ is at most the $k$-th
smallest of the other $n$ values. Then the number of covered placements is exactly $k$:
placement $i$ is covered if and only if the rank of $s_i$ among all $n+1$ values is at most $k$.
In particular the covered fraction is $k/(n+1)\ge 1-\alpha$, \emph{deterministically, for any
score vector whatsoever}.
\end{observation}

This is the whole finite-sample content of the guarantee. No mass is transported, no point is
steered; the balance that herding must manufacture by moving particles is already present in the
rank structure, and the proof merely rotates the pointer to exhibit it. Probability enters once,
at the end: exchangeability says the realized designation (slot $n+1$) is indistinguishable from a
uniform draw over the $n+1$ placements, so the deterministic fraction becomes a probability.

\section{The contragredient}\label{sec:contragredient}

The duality can be said precisely. The symmetric group acts on score vectors through permutation
matrices, $\rho(\pi)S=P_\pi S$; the contragredient (dual) representation acts on covectors,
$\rho^{*}(\pi)f = f\circ\rho(\pi^{-1})$, so that the pairing is invariant:
\[
\bigl\langle \rho^{*}(\pi)\,e_{n+1}^{*},\,\rho(\pi)\,S\bigr\rangle
=\bigl\langle e_{n+1}^{*},\,S\bigr\rangle .
\]
Every exchangeability proof of conformal validity can be run on either side of this pairing:
\emph{either} rearrange the data, $S\mapsto P_\pi S$, holding the test functional fixed,
\emph{or} hold the data fixed and pull the functional back,
$e_{n+1}^{*}\mapsto \rho^{*}(\pi^{-1})e_{n+1}^{*}=e_{\pi(n+1)}^{*}$. Because the quantile
threshold is a symmetric --- that is, invariant --- function of the score multiset, the two
descriptions agree event by event. The textbook proof is usually narrated in the first voice
(``swap the test point with a calibration point''); Observation~\ref{obs:count} is the second
voice, and the second voice is the one in which the mechanism is visible: the orbit being averaged
is the orbit of the \emph{functional}, $\{e_i^{*}\}_{i=1}^{n+1}$, not an orbit of datasets.

One honesty clause first: permutation representations are orthogonal, so the contragredient is
\emph{equivalent} to the original representation; the term is apt not because the dual carries
different structure but because it names which side of the pairing the action sits on --- the
covector side.

Where, then, is the steering? An earlier draft of this note claimed conformal performs none; that
is wrong, and the correction sharpens the duality. Write $p=1-\alpha$ and
$h_q(s)=\mathbf 1\{s\le q\}$ for the acceptance test at threshold $q$. Choosing the calibration
quantile $q_n=S_{(k)}$ with $k=\lceil p\,(n+1)\rceil$ enforces, up to ties, the identity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:steering}
0\;\le\;\sum_{i=1}^{n}\bigl(h_{q_n}(S_i)-p\bigr)\;<\;1
\qquad\text{(up to the $\pm1$ bookkeeping of the $(n+1)$-inflation),}
\end{equation}
and \eqref{eq:steering} is unmistakably a feedback-control identity: the realized scores determine
how the cutoff moves, and the cutoff moves so as to keep the accumulated acceptance-rate error
bounded by one unit --- the exact analogue of herding's bounded weight $\|w_n\|$, and the same
division by $n$ yields the same $1/n$. The difference between the two mechanisms is therefore not
steering versus no steering but \emph{which member of the pairing}
$\langle h,\nu\rangle=\int h\,d\nu$ is controlled: herding moves the empirical measure
$\nu_n=\tfrac1n\sum_i\delta_{x_i}$ against fixed tests $h=\phi$; conformal calibration moves the
test $h_{q_n}$ against the fixed empirical measure of scores. There are, in fact, two slot
movements in a conformal argument, and it is worth keeping them separate:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{rl}
\emph{steering:}& the acceptance boundary $q_n$ moves until the empirical rank count balances;\\[2pt]
\emph{transport:}& the proof moves the designation ``test'' among the slots (Observation~\ref{obs:count}).
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
Under exchangeability the transport is exact --- the pointer is equally entitled to indicate any
slot --- and the guarantee is the steering identity carried to the future slot intact. The
residual asymmetry with herding is only this: conformal does not control the generation of the
next observation. The steering is real, but it lives in the dual space of thresholds, acceptance
sets, and index labels rather than in the primal space of generated points:
\begin{center}
herding moves the observations to balance the measure;\\
conformal moves the acceptance boundary and the observation \emph{labels}\\
to balance --- and then transport --- an empirical rank count.
\end{center}

\section{The $1/n$ ledger, and dependence as damaged slots}\label{sec:ledger}

Both mechanisms produce $1/n$ rates, and for the same bookkeeping reason: an error that
accumulates \emph{boundedly} is divided by the sample size.
\begin{align*}
\text{herding:}\qquad
&\text{error}\;\le\;\frac{\text{bounded cumulative feature discrepancy }\|w_n\|}{n},\\[2pt]
\text{conformal:}\qquad
&\text{coverage damage}\;\le\;\frac{\text{number of damaged or deleted ranks}}{n}.
\end{align*}
The second line is not a metaphor; it is the proof technique of \citet{barberpananjady2026} for
split conformal on a dependent series. There, exact slot symmetry is unavailable: rotating the
test designation to slot $k$ is no longer distribution-preserving, because neighbouring scores
remember one another. The repair is to delete a buffer of $\tau$ slots around the splice before
rotating. Deleting $\tau$ of $n$ entries moves the $k$-th order statistic by at most $\tau$
ranks --- order statistics are insertion/deletion stable --- so the quantile level shifts by at
most $\tau/n$; and the residual failure of rotation invariance is charged at its total-variation
price, the switch coefficient $\bar\Psi_\tau$, bounded by $2\beta(\tau)$ for a stationary
$\beta$-mixing sequence. The coverage floor
\[
\Prob(\text{cover})\;\ge\;1-\alpha-\min_{\tau}\Bigl\{\underbrace{\tfrac{\tau}{n+1}}_{\text{slots
sacrificed}}+\underbrace{\bar\Psi_\tau}_{\text{failed invariance}}\Bigr\}
\]
is index-space low-discrepancy accounting --- in the decomposition above, the steering term is the
exact empirical rank correction and the switch term is the price of transporting that correction
to the future slot --- and nothing in it concentrates: no variance appears, no square root can. The contrast is instructive --- when the same problem is attacked by blocking
and empirical-process concentration \citep{oliveira2024}, the rate degrades to the Monte Carlo
$\sqrt{\tau/n}$, precisely because the counting structure has been traded away for a maximal
inequality. Herding's advantage over i.i.d.\ sampling and the switch coefficient's advantage over
blocking are the same advantage.

The quasi--Monte Carlo reading closes the loop. \citet{chen2010} present herding as a greedy
low-discrepancy construction: choose the next point to keep the running discrepancy bounded. The
rank sequence of any sample is, tautologically, a perfectly low-discrepancy sequence over its own
index set --- every rank appears exactly once --- and Observation~\ref{obs:count} is the
Koksma--Hlawka-style consequence: a test functional averaged over slots inherits the balance of
the ranks. Conformal prediction is what you get when the low-discrepancy sequence comes for free
and only the pointer needs transporting. The quadrature reading of conformal prediction is not
ours alone: \citet{snellgriffiths2025} recast the coverage computation as Bayesian quadrature over
the calibration scores, recovering the frequentist guarantee as a special case of a posterior over
test loss. The slot-transport view is the deterministic, index-space face of the same coin:
quadrature weights over scores on one side, a rotating evaluation functional over slots on the
other.

\section{What the duality does and does not explain}\label{sec:limits}

It explains the finite-sample ledger: why the guarantee is exact at finite $n$, why corrections
enter linearly in the number of damaged slots, and why dependence is a tax of size
(dependence range)/(calibration size) rather than its square root. It does not explain, and we do
not claim it explains, the statistical content: the step from ``$k$ of $n+1$ placements are
covered'' to ``the realized placement is covered with probability $k/(n+1)$'' is exchangeability,
or its quantitative surrogate under mixing, and no amount of slot bookkeeping supplies it. Nor
does dual-space steering create information: moving thresholds and labels over fixed scores can
re-level coverage exactly, but the conditional sharpness of the resulting intervals is fixed by
the scores themselves --- the information-gap point made elsewhere on this site
\citep{cotton_marginally}. A natural question is whether the steering can be aimed: a herding-like
feedback in index space that retains or reweights calibration slots to balance the rank count
\emph{conditionally}, not just marginally. Localized and weighted conformal methods are exactly
such aimed controllers, and the same information-theoretic ceiling applies to them. We leave the
speculation at one sentence.
