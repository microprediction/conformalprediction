Technical note

Herding as the contragredient of conformal prediction

Two 1/n mechanisms, dual to one another — and conformal came first.

Read the PDF LaTeX source

The result

Conformal calibration and herding each earn their \(O(1/n)\) rate from a cumulative discrepancy held bounded by an absolute constant: the quantile choice enforces \(\sum_i(\mathbf 1\{S_i\le q_n\}-p)\in[p,1+p)\), and herding’s weight recursion keeps \(\|w_n\|\) bounded. The note gives the two constructions parallel formal treatments and proves they control opposite arguments of a bilinear pairing between test functions and measures, \(\langle h,\nu\rangle=\int h\,d\nu\): herding steers the empirical measure against fixed tests; conformal steers the acceptance functional — and, in the validity proof, transports the evaluation index — against the fixed empirical measure of scores. The validity step itself is a deterministic counting identity: exactly \(\lceil(1-\alpha)(n+1)\rceil\) of the \(n+1\) placements of the evaluation index are covered, for any scores; exchangeability enters only to say the realized placement is an even draw.

Direction, and dependent data

Since the conformal mechanism (late 1990s) predates herding (2009), it is herding that realises the contragredient — the same ledger, moved to the primal space of generated points. The dual reading also prices dependence: in Barber–Pananjady’s time-series analysis, deleting \(\tau\) slots shifts the quantile level by at most \(\tau/(n+1)\) and the failed transport invariance is charged at total variation, \(\bar\Psi_\tau\le 2\beta(\tau)\). No concentration step appears, hence no square root — the same reason herding beats Monte Carlo.

What is and is not claimed

The duality is exact at the level of the finite-sample ledger and silent on the statistics: the step from “\(k\) of \(n{+}1\) placements are covered” to a probability is exchangeability, or its mixing surrogate, and slot bookkeeping does not supply it. And dual-space steering creates no information: thresholds and labels moving over fixed scores re-level coverage exactly, while the conditional sharpness stays with the scores — the information-gap point in another costume.

The companion demo runs both mechanisms side by side: kernel herding closing on its target at \(1/n\) against Monte Carlo's \(1/\sqrt n\), and the slot pointer rotating over a fixed, arbitrarily dependent score vector while the covered count sits at exactly \(k\) of \(n+1\).
