Conformal Prediction Provides a Rigorous Guarantee - That You'll Lose Money if You Rely on It
In recent years a technique called conformal prediction has been gathering mindshare in the machine learning community. It is frequently referred to as a more theoretically defensible means of attaching uncertainty to a prediction - one that comes with mathematical guarantees. 
I push back. Conformal prediction is statistically rigorous in a narrow, almost tautological sense: it rigorously delivers a marginal coverage accounting identity under exchangeability. But that rigor should not be confused with rigorous uncertainty modeling or for that matter, scientific or statistical rigor. 
This essay explains why with the help of a simple new theorem and some demonstrations created at conformalprediction.net. The theorem isolates cleanly what I term the information gap - the part part that conformal prediction will not, and cannot ever, help you with. 
The gap takes on the form of a Kullback-Liebler divergence - a quantity that comes with five different interpretations. Briefly: it quantifies the cost of conformal predictions' learned helplessness - an unfortunate deficit that users of conformal prediction deliberately sign up for. One of those interpretations: a direct translation to the rate at which you will lose money trading against less dogmatic opponents. 
The Core Observation
Let's start with what conformal prediction actually computes. You have a point predictor μ̂, a pile of calibration residuals r = y − μ̂(x), and a miscoverage level α. The recipe takes the order statistics of the absolute residuals and reads off the ⌈(n+1)(1−α)⌉-th one. That number is the half-width of every interval you emit. Add it, subtract it, done.
The "marginally useful" guarantee provided by conformal prediction. To apply conformal prediction in its simplest form is to conform to the unconditional empirical distribution when modeling our own residual errors. Yes that is better than, I don't know, assuming they follow the distribution implied by the New York City skyline for no particular reason- and some generous people would say the empirical distribution is not a terrible start. But in the same breadth, it is also morally equivalent, pretty much, to deciding not to model them at all.

There's certainly no arguing with this accounting, any more than one can argue with the definition of a cumulative distribution. Lovely. 
One notes, however, that this stance is philosophically inconsistent from the get-go. Nobody would demand that we conform to the empirical distribution for any arbitrary modeling task - that is evidently a complete abdication of responsibility. To do so leads us immediately to a slew of famously silly conclusions, such as assigning zero probability to any lottery combination that has never arisen in history; failing to account for regression to the mean in baseball hitting statistics mid season; not favoring recency in covariance estimation; and so on and so forth. 
So why then, is the task of modeling our model residuals to be considered a fundamentally different challenge, as compared with modeling anything else? Conformal prediction offers no response to that question, but I will, in the negative. Residual modeling is not so inherently different to modeling anything else that we should suspend our common sense, reach for the simplest rule we can find, and call it a day.

Because of this obvious flaw, conformal prediction as a field has been beating a hasty retreat from its very beginning. Yet to model residuals conditionally is to abandon the conformal guarantee. You are just modeling again and you can choose to label your method as "conformal" if you want but the interval immediately blows up and becomes worthless. The brand "conformal" is just a lobotomy because life has no guarantees. Use your noggin. 
Yes I'm sorry to be the bearer of bad news but you have to model residuals and think about it. There is no free lunch. If anything, trying to be "conformal first" is simply to impose a strong prior - somewhat ironic given that the conformal field has a way of attracting fanatical frequentists. Instead I suggest you make your model better in the first place to the point where a conformal wrapper doesn't add anything to your residuals. 
If your model is not well calibrated, calibrate it - using Platt or whatever you want. That's the easy part and if you don't believe me, just look at the bots participating at manifold prediction markets. Their calibration curves are visible on the site and they are near-perfect. Calibration is by far the easier part. Sharpness is difficult. You're playing against the sharps. If you don't know who the mug is … 
The Formal Cost of Unconditional Residual Modeling
The method is a sorted list and an index into it. That's it. The claimed mathematical insight is essentially synonymous with the definition of a cumulative distribution. There's no way on Earth that could ever transform the field of prediction - to think so its utterly absurd. This tautology certainly cannot constitute the be-all and end-all of better prediction. 
Forgive me if I flog this dead horse a little more. It does not look at the input x when it picks the width - it cannot, by construction. The width is one scalar reused for every test point. It is, in the most literal sense, a hardwired rule on the empirical distribution of residuals. You did not feed it anything about where the uncertainty is large and where it is small, so it cannot tell you. You handed it one number to cover everywhere, and it dutifully returns one number that covers everywhere - on average. Conformal prediction is the famous drunk who staggers down the road and is, on average in the middle of the road. Some safety guarantee!

Then, there is a second term that piles on too adding to the error of the best case - again, see the paper.  
I call this regret the conformal prediction information gap. And to repeat, conformalizing cannot do a thing about it. No re-leveling that ignores x can, because the gap is a property of the shape class, not of your calibration. In this demo you can hold a watch the score sit still while coverage snaps to target. If you want the spread to follow x, you have to put it there yourself, upstream, before the certificate goes on.

The irony is delicious. Conformal prediction is aggressively marketed as more rigorous - morally superior. This is sophistry. Rigor cuts both ways. Conformal prediction accidentally allows a rigorous definition of its inherent incurable inaccuracy, namely the information gap I(R;X). 
Fans would hold out hope that something can be done about this while maintaining the precious guarantee - but no luck. The conforal no-go theorems elucidate this. 
The real question, and the only germane one: how many ways we can interpret the information gap? The more ways we can, the more likely it is that conformal prediction enthusiasts will come to recognize the folly in viewing conformal prediction as "complete" in any sense. I will give you five interpretations, and perhaps you can invent more.  I built a live demo where one slider moves all five interpretations at once. 
1 - The cost of pretending everyone is the same.
We have already discussed the most obvious interpretation. A single-shape forecaster makes a quiet claim: once I have made my point prediction, the leftover error looks the same for every input - same spread for the easy cases and the hard ones. That is almost never true. Some inputs are noisy, some are calm. The gap is the bill for ironing them all flat into one average shape r̄. In symbols, I(R;X) = E_x KL( r(·|x) ‖ r̄ ): averaged over inputs, how far each input's true error law sits from the pooled one.
2 - An average log Bayes factor.
Take a single observation and ask: how much more likely was this residual under its true, x-specific law than under the pooled one? Log that ratio and you have a little vote for "the input mattered." Most single votes are small; average them and you get the gap. I(R;X) = E log[ r(R|x) / r̄(R) ] - the oracle's expected edge per data point, stated as evidence.
3 - The conformal ranks are not as uniform as they look. 
Conformal's party trick is just a distributional transform: it makes the rank U = G(R) come out uniform, so the histogram is a tidy flat bar. True - marginally. Split that same picture by input and the flatness evaporates. Easy inputs bunch their ranks near ½ (over-cautious there); hard inputs shove them to the tails (under-cautious). The flat bar was an average of lumpy conditional ones, and the size of that hidden lumpiness is, again, the gap. See the paper. 
4 - How far your world is from independence.
I(R;X) is, by definition, the KL distance from the real joint law of (input, residual) to the nearest world in which the two are independent. A single-shape model lives in that independent world - R ⊥ X is its founding assumption. So the gap is just the distance from where you are standing to where the truth actually is, and no amount of x-blind fiddling walks you across it. Re-leveling shuffles you around inside the independent world; it never leaves it.
5 - It is the rent. 
Finally, let's not forget the financial interpretation of KL distance, just in case anyone would be tempted to think this is all theoretical. In a fair parimutuel - equivalently, betting Kelly - a player who knows the true distribution P, wagering into a crowd that prices with some other Q, compounds wealth at a rate proportional to KL(P ‖ Q) per round. The informed player's edge is not vague hand-waving; it is a divergence, in nats. This is the heart of what I once called the lottery paradox : the rent an informed bettor extracts from the misinformed is KL.
Now point that lens at conformal prediction. The single-shape forecaster is the crowd - it prices every input with the same pooled residual law r̄. The oracle knows the conditional law r(·|x). The rent the oracle collects from the conformal forecaster, per observation, is KL( r(·|x) ‖ r̄ ) - and averaged over inputs, that is I(R;X).
So the gap is not an abstraction you can wave away. It is money. Each nat is a Kelly growth rate; divide by ln 2 and it is how many times the informed forecaster doubles its bankroll relative to yours, per observation. People who bet are not going to be fooled by conformal prediction as a brand! 
But the modern methods are sharp!
They are. Conformalized quantile regression, Mondrian and CRPS-binned predictive systems, conformal training - these produce genuinely sharp, shape-adaptive distributions, and they are standard practice now, not exotica. None of this contradicts the point above; it is the point above.
Look at where the sharpness comes from in each one. CQR fits a quantile model - that is the modeling. Mondrian fits a separate residual law per region - modeling. The CRPS-binned version chooses the bins by minimizing a proper score - that is conditional density estimation wearing a conformal hat. Conformal training differentiates through the conformalizer to minimize set size - an objective, optimized. 
In every case the work that buys the sharpness is the conditioning on x. The conformal step at the end supplies the coverage guarantee, which is real and worth having, and nothing else.
This is why I think the framing of these as "improvements to conformal prediction" is a joke. They are improvements to a model that happen to end with a conformal certificate. The certificate is the same order-statistic trick it always was. 
Attributing the benefit of modeling to the conformal accounting triviality is like giving Heinz credit for the engineering inside a Tesla because it is red. 
On moving the goalposts: time-series
The time-series story is the same. Drop exchangeability and the finite-sample guarantee is gone, so the literature re-defines what is guaranteed: marginal coverage becomes a long-run, time-average miscoverage frequency (ACI), or a regret bound over local intervals (online conformal under arbitrary shift). These are ingenious, and - this matters - long-run frequency control under drift is a genuine, hard-won engineering guarantee. Often it is the most you can ask for without a model of the shift.
Coverage now, not on average: fixed split conformal collapses once a volatility regime arrives; adaptive methods and a simple probabilistic volatility model track the targetBut notice it is a weaker, differently posed object than the thing you usually want, which is a sharp predictive distribution now, for this step. Trading "covered here" for "covered 95% of the time on average over the run" is a fine trade when the application genuinely demands a coverage certificate. It is not a fine trade when you talked yourself into it because the original guarantee evaporated and the word "conformal" was too good to give up. Redefine the goalposts when the application moves them - not to keep the brand.

When conformal prediction is exactly the right tool
None of this is anti-conformal, to those who actually understand what conformal prediction entails (and what it doesn't). 
There is a clean set of jobs where coverage or containment is the legitimate desire, and there the distribution-free, finite-sample guarantee is exactly the value added. I've made an effort to enumerate good examples on the site. Just don't confuse them for improving distributional prediction in a way that is helpful - say in a betting, investing, market-making or even weather-forecasting context. 
Anomaly and novelty detection via conformal p-values 
Might anomaly or novelty detection provide conformal prediction's most natural home, where it is a distribution-free hypothesis test and coverage is Type-I error control? You decide. See the demo. 
But in general I would remain wary of "anomaly detection" applications. No-one has ever been able to usefully define the term. I have argued that only a market can provide a reasonable definition of anomaly for time-series (per my article). As prediction markets flood the world, this view is likely to become more commonplace.
